Narrative Engine: A Technical Design for Qualitative Historical Forecasting

Status: Draft v0.1

Concept: Treat history as a corpus of stories with recurring arcs. Use LLMs to extract, classify, and structure those arcs at multiple time scales, then generate forecasts ("theses") by asking: given where we are in the story, how do stories like this usually end?

Problem Statement & Requirements

Quantitative forecasting models consume prices, dates, and time series. This system instead consumes text — histories, articles, primary sources — and builds a structured, semantic model of how situations evolve. The core bet is that narrative arcs are non-random and that pattern-matching a present situation against a library of historical arcs yields predictive signal.

Core Conceptual Model

The data model is the heart of the system. Three primitives:

Episode — the atomic narrative unit. A bounded stretch of historical action with a beginning state, a tension, and a resolution. Example: "Weimar hyperinflation, 1921–1923."

Arc — an archetypal shape an episode instantiates. The taxonomy includes: rise-and-overextension, hubris-nemesis, reform-then-reaction, decadence-and-renewal, siege-and-collapse, succession crisis, credit-boom-and-bust, generational forgetting.

Cycle — a recursive container. Cycles nest: a civilizational cycle contains institutional cycles contains generational cycles contains episodes.

Historical Example: The 1929 Stock Market Crash

This episode instantiates the credit-boom-and-bust arc, with clear phases:
- Boom (1922-1927): Post-war economic expansion
- Euphoria (1927-1929): Speculative excess, margin trading
- Distress (October 1929): Initial cracks, Black Thursday
- Panic (October 1929): Cascade selling, Black Tuesday
- Revulsion (1929-1932): Market bottom, 89% decline

Initiating Conditions:
- Speculative excess in stock market
- Widespread margin trading (10% down)
- Weak economic fundamentals hidden by optimism

Escalation Mechanics:
- Panic selling cascades
- Margin calls forcing liquidation
- Loss of confidence in banking system

Resolution:
- Market bottomed July 1932
- Dow declined 89% from peak
- Great Depression followed

Consequences:
- Banking crisis (1930-1933)
- New Deal reforms enacted
- Keynesian economics ascendant
- Bretton Woods system (post-WWII)

Actor: Herbert Hoover (President, Republican, 1929-1933)
- Role: Failed to stem panic
- Attributes: Believed in limited government intervention

Another Example: Weimar Hyperinflation (1921-1923)

This episode shows fiscal distress and currency collapse.

Initiating Conditions:
- War reparations burden (Treaty of Versailles)
- Occupation of Ruhr (1923)
- Passive resistance funded by money printing

Escalation:
- Currency velocity accelerated
- Wage-price spirals
- Savings destroyed

Resolution:
- Rentenmark introduced (Nov 1923)
- Currency stabilized
- Political radicalization followed

Key Design Principles

1. Schema-first extraction: LLM fills fixed schema
2. Taxonomy as data: Arc definitions in versioned config
3. Provenance required: Every claim traceable to source
4. Mechanism vocabulary: elite_overproduction, fiscal_distress, immiseration
5. Inferred links flagged: Structural similarity without explicit reference
6. Human review: Inferred links above threshold reviewed

Framework Imports

External frameworks can be imported as versioned packages:
- Dalio (economic cycles)
- Turchin (structural-demographic)
- Strauss-Howe (generational)
- I Ching (64 situation-states)

Evaluation

Masked-ending backtests: Feed sources up to year T, hide after, ask for thesis, score against actual outcomes.

Leakage control is critical: The LLM may already know how WWI ended. Mitigations include scoring analog selection separately, using obscure histories, and post-cutoff events.

Calibration: Track Brier scores over resolved theses.

Risks

- Narrative fallacy: Over-recording dramatic arcs
- Historiographic bias: Corpus encodes author theories
- Taxonomy validity: Arc sets are hypotheses
- Leakage: LLM training data contamination
